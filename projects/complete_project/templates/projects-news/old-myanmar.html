Affected Population: The cyclone hit the Southeast Asian nation early on Saturday, May 3. Parents have told stories of having children ripped from their arms by the ferocious winds and swirling tides. More than 77,000 people have been confirmed dead and another 55,000 people are missing. Many observers expect the death toll to continue to rise because some of the hardest hit areas along the Irrawaddy River are still inaccessible.


HISG Relief Effort: HISG sent staff into Myanmar and helped build a coalition of local organizations working together for cyclone relief. The coalition has been recognized by the junta government of Myanmar and is allowed to receive and distribute international aid. This partnership is one of the few organizations to be given such permission.


Secondly, HISG coordinated medical teams that set up clinics in different parts of the city of Yangon, treating over 600 patients in a matter of days. The medical teams also rebuilt orphanages around the city that had been damaged by the storm. Click here for video from the trip.


Third, HISG staff trained Myanmar nationals from Southeast Asia in large-scale disaster relief. This training is especially critical because the government of Myanmar has been extremely reluctant to allow foreign aid workers into the country. The Myanmar nationals will have easier access to the disaster zone.


Fourth, HISG shipped hundreds of water purification systems for distribution in areas where no clean water was available. The water filters are easy to install and simple to maintain, and each one can provide enough water for an entire family for 12 months. In addition to the water filters, HISG provided several hand pumps that can be installed in a few hours and will reach down to underground sources of drinkable water. HISG delivered 200 bags of rice to the villages along with the water pumps. The rice was used for food, as well as seed for farmers whose crops were ruined by the massive swells of salt water during the cyclone.


Finally, HISG purchased property to establish an orphan and foster care facility in the Irrawaddy Delta region for the children who lost their homes and families in the storm. HISG also purchased goats to be raised on the property. The sales of milk and offspring from the goats will make the project self-sustaining and fund the orphan care program. Four children (pictured) are already living at the facility.


The scope of these projects ranges from immediate disaster relief to caring for the most vulnerable children in the community, but one common thread is that most of them empower local people to reach out to their communities. The orphan care program, the relief coalition, and the disaster response training are all examples of how HISG is able to connect to local networks and contacts to maximize the impact of each project. Urgent Needs: Medicine and medical supplies are much needed. In 2000 the World Health Organization ranked Myanmar's overall health care system as the second worst in the world. Food is another need, as prices have skyrocketed, with some staples tripling in cost. Many communities desperately need clean water and water purifiers because thousands of displaced people are living in close quarters where infectious diseases are easily spread. There is also an urgent need for tents and other temporary shelters for all the families whose homes have been destroyed. Aside from these physical needs there are other challenges for aid organizations. Communication is a problem because most powerlines and phone lines have been knocked down. Transportation is also difficult because of fuel shortages, blocked roads, and many of the smaller boats that would be used to reach flooded areas were destroyed by the cyclone.


HISG Presence: HISG has strong relationships with local aid organizations in Southeast Asia. We were heavily involved in relief projects after the 2004 Asian Tsunami. Our warehouse sent several containers to affected areas of Indonesia and outfitted a hospital near ground zero. HISG has also helped start small businesses to assist in the community development of the affected areas. In addition, HISG staff has taught disaster response in Malaysia, Singapore, the Philippines and Thailand, which neighbors Myanmar.
